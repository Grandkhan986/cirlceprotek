The Global Positioning System (GPS) was originally developed by the U.S. Department of Defense in the 1970s, primarily for military navigation. The system utilized a network of satellites to provide precise location data to ground forces. By the early 1990s, GPS became available for civilian use, marking a pivotal moment in how we could navigate our world. This transition sparked the development of various applications, including personal long-distance GPS trackers.

Fast forward to the 2000s, and the technology began to trickle down into consumer products. Companies like Garmin and TomTom pioneered portable GPS devices, enabling users to never lose track of their routes while driving. The introduction of smartphone technology further revolutionized this space. With apps like Google Maps and Waze, people could not only find their way but also keep tabs on loved ones and pets.

  • In 2005, the first GPS-enabled smartphone, the Nokia 6710 Navigator, hit the market, allowing users to navigate and locate others seamlessly.

The Camino de Santiago, known as the Way of St. James, has been a pilgrimage since the 9th century when the remains of St. James were believed to have been discovered in Santiago de Compostela. This discovery transformed the region into a spiritual hub, attracting thousands of pilgrims each year. Today, approximately 300,000 individuals undertake this journey annually, many of whom utilize GPS technology to navigate the sometimes challenging terrain.

As you walk the Camino, you'll encounter various historical landmarks and sites that tell the story of its significance. Here are some key aspects that illustrate how this pilgrimage has evolved:

  • The Pilgrim’s Office: Established in Santiago, this office has been issuing the “Compostela,” a certificate of completion, to pilgrims for centuries, adding a sense of accomplishment to your journey.
  • Medieval Structures: The route is dotted with ancient churches, monasteries, and bridges, such as the iconic Romanesque bridge in Puente La Reina, showcasing the architectural evolution throughout the ages.
  • Cultural Heritage: The Camino has been recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage site, emphasizing the importance of preserving its historical integrity while allowing modern pilgrims to transform their journey.
  • Modern Technology: Apps like Camino de Santiago GPS and other digital tools have emerged, providing accurate navigation while respecting the tradition of the pilgrimage.
